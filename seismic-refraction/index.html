Seismic Tomography Surveys in Chilliwack: Refraction & Reflection for Subsurface Imaging

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

LEARN MORE →

The geophone spread gets laid out across the gravel bar, a 24-channel line with 5-metre spacing, and the weight drop hits the strike plate — that sharp, percussive thump sends P-waves rippling through the unconsolidated valley fill that defines much of Chilliwack's subsurface. Within milliseconds, first arrivals register on the seismograph, and the refraction profile starts to resolve. Seismic tomography takes that raw travel-time data and inverts it into a velocity model, revealing layers, anomalies, and the depth to competent bedrock. In a place like Chilliwack, where the Fraser River has deposited metres of loose alluvium over glacial till and where the Vedder fan spreads coarse sediment across the southern flats, knowing what lies beneath is not optional — it is the difference between a foundation that performs and one that surprises you during excavation. We run both refraction and reflection surveys depending on the target depth and resolution required, often pairing seismic tomography with MASW profiling to constrain shear-wave velocities for site classification under the National Building Code of Canada.

A single seismic refraction line across the Vedder fan can resolve four distinct velocity layers before hitting bedrock — information that twenty boreholes alone would struggle to interpolate.

Our approach and scope

Chilliwack sits in a complex geological transition — the Cascade foothills rise abruptly to the south and east, while the Fraser lowlands extend northward across thick sequences of post-glacial sediment. Depth to bedrock varies dramatically: less than 5 metres near the base of Chilliwack Mountain, but exceeding 150 metres in the central valley where the Ryder Lake basin deepens beneath Quaternary fill. This contrast makes seismic tomography particularly valuable here. The refraction method maps the compressional-wave velocity structure of the subsurface, identifying the boundary between unconsolidated overburden and higher-velocity bedrock, while reflection processing images deeper stratigraphic horizons. Our field crews work with 48-channel systems when the site geometry allows, using both hammer-and-plate sources for shallow targets and accelerated weight drops or small explosive charges for deeper penetration. We process the data through iterative ray-tracing inversion algorithms that account for lateral velocity variations — critical in the Vedder Crossing area where channel deposits create sharp impedance contrasts that simpler methods miss. The resulting tomograms provide a continuous cross-section of compressional-wave velocities, which our engineers interpret alongside borehole control from SPT drilling to calibrate the geophysical response against measured penetration resistance.

Site-specific factors

What we keep seeing in Chilliwack is the hidden velocity inversion — a layer of loose, low-velocity sand sitting beneath a denser, higher-velocity crust that forms from desiccation or compaction near the surface. Standard refraction interpretation assumes velocity increases monotonically with depth, so that soft layer gets masked, and the interpreted bedrock depth comes out shallower than reality. On a warehouse pad near the Chilliwack River, this exact scenario added six figures to earthworks costs when excavation hit unexpected loose material at four metres. Reflection surveying helps catch these blind zones because it images interfaces directly rather than inferring them from velocity gradients. Another local concern is the water table — perched groundwater within the Vedder fan creates a strong reflection that can be misinterpreted as bedrock by less experienced processors. We cross-check every seismic tomogram against available borehole logs and, where liquefaction potential is a factor under NBCC seismic hazard values for the Fraser Valley, we integrate the velocity data into a site-specific ground model that feeds directly into the foundation design and seismic site classification.

Top questions

What is the difference between seismic refraction and reflection, and which one do I need for my Chilliwack site?

Refraction measures the time it takes for seismic waves to travel down to a velocity boundary and back up to the surface — it works well for mapping bedrock depth and rippability when velocities increase with depth. Reflection records energy that bounces off subsurface interfaces, similar to sonar, and images both velocity increases and decreases, making it better for deep stratigraphy and detecting buried channels. For most Chilliwack sites with less than 40 metres of overburden, refraction gives you what you need efficiently. When the target is deeper — say the Ryder Lake basin or a fault investigation — we run reflection surveys. Often we combine both on the same spread to get shallow velocity control plus deep structural imaging.

How much does a seismic tomography survey cost in Chilliwack?

Survey costs run between CA$3,690 and CA$7,210 depending on the line length, number of spreads, source type, and whether you need refraction only or combined refraction/reflection. A single 115-metre refraction line with 24 geophones and a weight-drop source falls at the lower end. A multi-line survey with 48 channels, explosive sources for deep penetration, and full tomographic processing sits at the upper end. Every quote includes mobilization within the Fraser Valley, data acquisition, processing, and a signed engineering report with interpreted cross-sections.

Can seismic tomography detect faults in the Chilliwack area?

Yes — reflection tomography is one of the best geophysical tools for imaging near-surface faulting. The Vedder Fault and associated splays of the Sumas Fault system cut through Quaternary sediments in the Chilliwack region, and we have mapped vertical offsets of less than a metre at depths of 30 to 60 metres using SH-wave reflection profiling. The key is high spatial sampling and careful static corrections, especially on the sloping ground near the mountain front where elevation changes can distort travel times. We tie all fault interpretations back to available geological mapping from the BC Geological Survey and, where possible, confirm with targeted boreholes.

How long does a seismic tomography survey take, and what access do you need?

A single 115-metre refraction line typically takes half a day of field work, including setup, shooting, and preliminary quality control. Multi-line or reflection surveys run one to three days depending on coverage. We need a cleared strip roughly two metres wide along the line; heavy brush and standing water degrade data quality, so winter surveys on frozen ground or late-summer surveys after hay cutting often produce the best results in Chilliwack's agricultural areas. The weight drop is mounted on a small tracked carrier that fits through a standard gate, and we use hand-carried equipment for steep or sensitive terrain.

What deliverables do I get from a seismic tomography investigation?

You receive a signed, stamped engineering report that includes the survey layout plan with GPS coordinates, raw and processed seismograms, the 2D velocity tomogram with interpreted horizons, a depth-to-bedrock profile along the line, and — when part of a seismic site classification — the calculated Vs30 and NBCC site class. We also provide the digital velocity model in formats that your structural engineer can import directly into foundation design software. Turnaround on the report is typically seven to ten working days after field acquisition, sooner if the project schedule demands it.
