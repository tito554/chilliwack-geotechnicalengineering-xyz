Grouting Design in Chilliwack: Permeation and Jet Grouting for Variable Alluvial Soils

The Fraser River has been depositing and reworking sediments across the Chilliwack area for millennia, leaving behind a complex stratigraphy of coarse gravel lenses, silty overbank deposits, and occasional cobble-rich layers that frustrate conventional foundation solutions. When a condominium excavation near Vedder Road encountered uncontrolled groundwater inflow through a buried channel filled with open-framework gravel, the contractor learned quickly that dewatering alone would not stabilize the cut. Grouting design in Chilliwack requires moving beyond rule-of-thumb approaches and toward a targeted methodology calibrated to the specific grain-size distribution and hydraulic conductivity of each unit. For projects where the treatment zone extends into finer silts, the team often coordinates with in-situ permeability testing to refine grout penetration predictions before mobilization, ensuring the selected mix rheology matches the formation without excessive travel or premature setup.

Effective grouting design matches grout rheology to the formation's pore geometry: too thick and penetration halts at the borehole wall, too thin and material escapes beyond the target treatment zone.

Our approach and scope

The NBCC 2020 references CSA A23.3 for structural concrete, but the design of a grouting program itself draws heavily on ASTM and FHWA guidelines adapted to local hydrogeologic conditions. In Chilliwack, the distinction between alluvial fan deposits near the Vedder Mountain front and the finer floodplain sediments closer to the Fraser main stem defines two fundamentally different grouting problems: the former demands high-mobility microfine cements capable of penetrating clean sands with D15 values below 0.3 mm, while the latter often requires fracture-based permeation or limited soil mixing where discrete seepage paths dominate. A properly engineered program specifies refusal criteria, maximum injection pressures to avoid hydrofracture, and a staged drilling sequence that uses primary-secondary-tertiary hole patterns to progressively tighten the curtain. For sites where the grouted mass must also resist lateral spreading during a seismic event, the design parameters are cross-checked against liquefaction assessment results derived from SPT or CPT data collected on the same footprint.

Site-specific factors

The grouting rig positioned on a Chilliwack site is typically a compact track-mounted unit equipped with a high-speed colloidal mixer and a positive-displacement piston pump capable of delivering steady flow at pressures exceeding 4 MPa. The pump's stroke counter and real-time pressure transducer feed data to an onboard acquisition system that logs volume, flow rate, and apparent Lugeon values at each stage, giving the superintendent an immediate view of grout take versus the theoretical envelope. In the Fraser Valley, the primary risk during permeation grouting is not inadequate penetration but rather uncontrolled communication with open-grade layers that channel grout far beyond the intended treatment zone, wasting material and potentially heaving adjacent infrastructure. Monitoring surface elevation through laser levels or tiltmeters around the injection grid is standard protocol; a rapid rise in grout take without a corresponding pressure increase usually signals a connection to a high-conductivity feature and requires an immediate switch to a thicker mix or a reduced pumping rate.

Top questions

What is the typical cost range for grouting design on a Chilliwack commercial project?

Design fees for a grouting program in the Chilliwack area generally fall between CA$1,720 and CA$5,830 depending on the treatment volume, number of boreholes, and the complexity of the subsurface profile. A simple curtain for a single-family basement excavation sits at the lower end, while a multi-row jet grouting scheme beneath a commercial building with extensive QA/QC requirements approaches the upper bound.

How does the Fraser Valley alluvium affect grout selection?

The alluvium across Chilliwack contains a wide range of particle sizes, from clean gravels near Vedder Mountain to silty sands on the floodplain. The design must match grout particle size to the formation's D15 using the well-established criterion that the grout's D85 should be smaller than 9 to 15 times the soil's D15 for effective permeation; where silt content exceeds 15%, permeation gives way to fracture grouting or jet grouting as the dominant mechanism.

What QA/QC testing is performed during a grouting program?

Quality control includes continuous real-time monitoring of pressure, flow rate, and cumulative volume against the theoretical grout take envelope. Post-grouting verification typically involves core drilling through treated zones for visual inspection and unconfined compressive strength testing, combined with Lugeon or packer permeability tests in the curtain to confirm the target hydraulic conductivity has been achieved.

Can grouting stabilize excavations below the groundwater table in Chilliwack?

Yes, a properly designed permeation or jet grouting curtain can create a low-permeability barrier that reduces inflow to manageable levels, often eliminating the need for continuous deep-well dewatering. The key is identifying the depth and continuity of the high-conductivity gravel seams through detailed site investigation before finalizing the grout hole geometry and injection sequence.

What is the difference between permeation grouting and jet grouting?

Permeation grouting injects a fluid grout into the pore space of the soil without disturbing its structure, making it suitable for sands and gravels where the grout can flow through interconnected voids. Jet grouting uses high-velocity fluid jets to erode and mix the in-situ soil with cementitious grout, creating a soil-cement column that works well in silts and clays where permeation is not feasible due to small pore throats.
